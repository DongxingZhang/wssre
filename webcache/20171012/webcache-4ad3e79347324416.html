Consumer prices increased 2.6% y-o-y in August, notably accelerating from 2.2%in July and surprising the market on the upside. However, we note that fasterinflation was largely due to a 18% y-o-y jump in fresh food prices and somepositive base effect from lower utility prices last year. Indeed, underlying pricesmeasures remained stable. The central bank's preferred measure of core inflation,excluding food and energy prices, actually decelerated to 1.4% y-o-y in Augustafter staying at 1.5% y-o-y for three straight months. We expect headline inflationto decelerate in 4Q17, as the base effect in energy prices washes out.

The headline consumer price index (CPI) increased 2.6% y-o-y (HSBC: 2.3, BBG: 2.2) inAugust, accelerating notably from 2.2% in July and marking the highest level of growth sinceearly 2012. Seasonally adjusted by HSBC, CPI increased 0.3% m-o-m sa in August, followinga similar gain in July.

Headline price gains were largely driven by a sharp rise in fresh food prices, reflectingunfavourable weather conditions. Indeed, prices of fresh foods increased 18.3% y-o-y (or+3.3% m-o-m sa) in August, led largely by vegetable and fruit prices. Meanwhile, the baseeffect from energy prices also contributed to y-o-y price gains due to temporary electricitytariff cuts in 3Q17 last year.

Excluding food and energy prices, the BoK's preferred measure of core CPI remained largelyunchanged, decelerating to 1.4% y-o-y in August after staying at 1.5% for the previous threemonths. Core CPI, excluding agriculture and oil prices, was also kept unchanged at 1.8% y-o-y(HSBC: 1.7, BBG: 1.8) in August. Indeed, service prices are showing few signs ofaccelerating, in both public and private services.
